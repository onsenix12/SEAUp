# The Problem

## 7 Million People. Near-Zero Economic Participation.

Across ASEAN's 690 million people, an estimated 7 million live with intellectual disabilities (ID). Indonesia alone accounts for 2.8 million — a population larger than many countries, yet almost entirely absent from the formal economy. In Indonesia, 91% of employed persons with disabilities work informally. For those with intellectual disabilities specifically, competitive employment is not rare — it is essentially nonexistent.

This is not a problem of capability. It is a problem of exclusion by design.

## A System That Was Never Built for Them

ASEAN governments have frameworks on paper. Indonesia's 2016 Disability Law mandates 2% PWD hiring in government and 1% in private companies. The ASEAN Enabling Masterplan 2025 established 76 action points across all member states. Yet the mid-term review found persistent failures in awareness, coordination, and enforcement. Kerjabilitas.com, Indonesia's leading disability employment platform, has 12,000 registered users and places roughly 100 people per year. For a population of 2.8 million, that is not a solution — it is a footnote.

Singapore, the most developed disability ecosystem in the region, still records only 34.7% employment among working-age people with disabilities. For intellectual disabilities, the dominant pathway remains sheltered workshops — environments that provide structure but not dignity, activity but not income, presence but not participation.

## The Creative Economy Excludes Them Too

Disability art organisations globally have proven for decades that people with intellectual disabilities produce work of genuine creative and commercial value. HERALBONY in Japan generates millions licensing ID artwork to Japan Airlines, Nikon, and Toyota. JOURNEY in Singapore partners with UOB, CapitaLand, and Gardens by the Bay. The demand for this work is real and growing — corporate DE&I budgets are expanding, and ASEAN consumers show significantly higher willingness to pay for inclusive brands than their European counterparts.

Yet in ASEAN, no equivalent platform exists at scale. Indonesia has no disability art organisation operating beyond a local, analog level. The Philippines has scattered initiatives reaching dozens of people. Singapore's most developed programs remain physically constrained to studios serving tens, not thousands.

The creative economy is growing rapidly across ASEAN. ID individuals are not in it.

## Current User Journey: Where the System Breaks Down

To understand the depth of this problem, consider what the journey looks like today for an ID individual in ASEAN who wants to express their creativity and earn from it.

```
STAGE 1 — AWARENESS
"I want to create something."

The ID individual has creative impulse — a colour they love, a feeling
they want to express, a world they imagine. But there is no platform,
no pathway, no prompt that says: your creativity has value here.

Pain point: No accessible entry point exists. No tool is designed for them.
In Indonesia, nothing exists in Bahasa Indonesia at all.

         ↓

STAGE 2 — ATTEMPTING EXISTING TOOLS
"Maybe I can use an AI art tool."

They — or a caregiver on their behalf — encounter Midjourney, DALL-E,
or Adobe Firefly. These tools require precise English text prompts,
sustained reading comprehension, and iterative refinement across
multiple attempts.

Pain point: Immediate exclusion. Text-based prompting assumes literacy,
language fluency, and abstract thinking — skills many ID individuals
do not have. The tool was never designed for them.

         ↓

STAGE 3 — FALLING BACK ON INSTITUTIONS
"Maybe a day programme can help."

The caregiver seeks out a disability day programme or sheltered workshop.
Some run art sessions. Staff spend 4+ hours weekly planning activities,
80% report idea fatigue, and resources are stretched thin.

Pain point: Art produced in these settings rarely leaves the room.
There is no pipeline from creation to market. Staff lack the time,
tools, and training to bridge that gap. The artwork exists — and
then disappears.

         ↓

STAGE 4 — THE INCOME DEAD END
"Can this become something?"

Even when artwork is created, monetisation requires: a digital
storefront, product photography, print-on-demand logistics, marketing,
payment processing, and legal IP management. No disability org in
ASEAN's ID space has assembled this infrastructure at scale.

Pain point: The creative output exists but cannot reach a buyer.
The distance between "art made" and "income earned" is unbridgeable
with current resources.

         ↓

STAGE 5 — OUTCOME
"This wasn't for me."

The ID individual returns to passivity. The caregiver returns to
routine programming. The artwork is pinned to a wall, or discarded.
The cycle repeats next week.

Pain point: No accumulation of value. No identity as a creator.
No income. No dignity that comes from economic contribution.
```

This journey is not unique to one person. It is the default experience for the overwhelming majority of ASEAN's 7 million people with intellectual disabilities. The system does not fail them at one point — it fails them at every point.

## AI Has Made This Worse, Not Better

The emergence of generative AI tools — Midjourney, Adobe Firefly, DALL-E — promised to democratise creative production. Instead, they have deepened exclusion. These platforms require precise text prompting, sustained digital literacy, and iterative refinement. Research confirms that AI currently functions as a multiplier of existing competence, not a substitute for it (Gusta & Ramanathan, 2025). For people with intellectual disabilities, who face barriers in language processing, abstract reasoning, and sustained attention, these tools are inaccessible by design.

The result: the fastest-growing creative technology in history is widening the gap between people with intellectual disabilities and the digital economy — at precisely the moment when that gap was becoming impossible to ignore.

Meanwhile, the Center for Digital Society at UGM confirmed in 2024 that virtually no generative AI applications for people with disabilities exist in Bahasa Indonesia. The largest ID population in ASEAN has no tools, no platforms, and no pathways into the digital creative economy.

---

## How Might We?

**How might we design an AI-powered creative system that is accessible enough for people with intellectual disabilities to use independently or with light support — so that their creative output can reach real markets, generate real income, and flow back into the communities that need it most?**

This is not a question about technology alone. It is a question about whether the digital economy will be designed to include everyone — or only those already equipped to navigate it.
